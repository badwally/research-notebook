## Open Problems

**Unsolved Mechanisms and Neurobiological Gaps**
Despite significant observations regarding neuroprotection and systemic health, the exact biological mechanisms driving many of these benefits remain unsolved. For instance, while GLP-1 therapies show promise in treating chronic headaches and migraines, the specific pathways involved are still under investigation and lack definitive proof [1, 2]. Furthermore, the mechanistic role of GLP-1 receptor agonists in modulating resting-state brain functional connectivity has been inadequately examined [3]. 

There is also a critical gap in long-term data regarding how chronic GLP-1 signaling affects the body and brain over decades. Because these drugs systematically dampen dopamine tone, researchers are raising questions about whether long-term dopamine suppression might eventually blunt a patient's natural curiosity, creativity, and drive [4]. Additionally, the long-term systemic effects on reproductive hormones, such as luteinizing hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH)—especially in younger users—remain an unsolved problem requiring further study [4].

**Methodological Limitations and Clinical Challenges**
A major limitation of current research into the broader systemic, neurocognitive, and psychotropic benefits of GLP-1s is that human findings are often based on small, methodologically heterogeneous studies with short follow-up periods [5]. Furthermore, much of the systemic data relies on retrospective observational models, such as electronic health record analyses. Because these methods are observational, they can only establish associations—such as the link between tirzepatide and a lower risk of erectile dysfunction—rather than confirming direct cause-and-effect relationships [6]. 

From a clinical and physiological standpoint, a massive challenge remains in mitigating the loss of lean muscle mass. Up to 40% of the weight lost on high-efficacy GLP-1 therapies can consist of lean body mass, which can include significant organ shrinkage (e.g., the liver shrinking by nearly half) [7, 8]. This poses a severe risk to the physical independence and systemic health of older patients, particularly because most patients currently receive almost no nutritional counseling while taking the medications [8].

**Future Research Directions**
To address these limitations, the sources outline several distinct avenues for future research:

*   **Robust Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs):** Researchers emphasize the urgent need for large-scale, robust randomized trials utilizing standardized psychotropic and systemic endpoints to move beyond observational data and definitively confirm the efficacy and mechanisms of GLP-1s for conditions like erectile dysfunction, cognitive impairment, and mood disorders [5, 6].
*   **Transdiagnostic and Multi-Agonist Therapeutics:** Future development is heavily focused on exploring dual agonists (such as GLP-1/GIP or GLP-1/PYY3-36) and triple agonists to enable synergistic effects at lower doses and mitigate undesirable side effects [9]. These multi-agonists are being evaluated as "transdiagnostic mechanistically informed therapeutics" targeting a broad spectrum of major neurocognitive and psychiatric conditions, including Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, traumatic brain injury, and depressive disorders [10]. 
*   **Mapping Specific Neurocircuitry:** There is a mandate to precisely examine the neural circuits and networks affected by GLP-1 receptor activity, to understand exactly how they modulate cognitive and psychopathological domains in mental and metabolic disorders [11].
*   **Longevity and the Biology of Aging:** Because GLP-1 pathways have been shown to reduce systemic inflammation, improve mitochondrial function, and support DNA repair, a major frontier for future research is investigating whether these medications function as fundamental "longevity drugs" capable of directly targeting the biological hallmarks of aging itself [12].
